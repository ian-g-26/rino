ORIGINS

Background Information

What we now call the River North Art District wasn't colorful or creative at all. It was loud, gritty, and built around the railroad tracks and the South Platte River. Warehouses, rail yards, and factories filled the area, and most of the people nearby were working class laborers tied to Denver's growing mining and transportation economy.


It wasn't a place people went for murals or breweries. It was a place people went to work. The bones of that industrial past — especially the brick buildings and rail lines — are still what shape the district today.

A DISTRICT TRANSFORMED

The Timeline

1882

Railroads & Industry

RiNo's growth began around 1871, when Denver expanded north along new railroad lines. The area became a hub for warehouses, stockyards, smelters, and manufacturing, especially tied to agriculture and mining. Its location near rail corridors made it ideal for moving goods, not for housing, so it developed as a mostly industrial district with working-class labor nearby.

INDUSTRIAL ERA
1928

Decline & Disinvestment

After World War II, many industries left or modernized elsewhere, and rail use declined. Factories closed, buildings were abandoned, and the area saw economic disinvestment. Zoning kept it industrial, which limited residential or commercial reinvestment and left many large brick warehouses underused.

POST-WAR DECLINE
1990s

The Art Began

Cheap rents and big open buildings attracted artists and makers looking for studio space. Murals, galleries, and informal creative spaces started appearing, slowly shifting the district's identity from purely industrial to arts-driven reuse. This is when the name RiNo Art District began to stick.

CREATIVE REUSE
2019

Rapid Redevelopment

Once the arts scene gained attention, developers followed. Breweries, restaurants, apartments, and offices moved in, and many old warehouses were converted into mixed-use buildings. Public art became a defining feature, but so did rising rents and displacement, pushing out many of the artists and longtime industrial businesses that helped spark the revival.

URBAN REVIVAL
THE PRESENT

Today's Growth

The creative energy that drew artists to RiNo continues to shape the district. Murals, galleries, and informal creative spaces remain central to its identity, even as the neighborhood matures into a more complex urban environment with mixed-use development, dining, and nightlife.

LOOKING AHEAD

20 Years Into the Future

The River North Art District will likely feel like a fully built-out urban neighborhood rather than a former warehouse district. From an urban planning perspective, it will probably be denser and more vertical, with more mid-rise and high-rise housing mixed with offices and retail.

Denser & More Vertical

More mid-rise and high-rise housing mixed with offices and retail, transforming the skyline while respecting the industrial bones of the neighborhood.

Walkable & Connected

Streets will prioritize walkability, bikes, and transit over cars, with wider sidewalks, better crossings, and stronger connections to the South Platte River.

Public Spaces & Riverfront

Public spaces and riverfront parks will play a bigger role in tying the neighborhood together, creating gathering places for all residents.

If done well, RiNo will evolve into a fully realized mixed-use district that is dense, walkable, and lived in at all hours, but still grounded in its industrial framework. The brick warehouses, rail alignments, and gritty scale will not disappear. Instead, they will serve as the structural backbone for a more mature urban neighborhood that feels layered rather than replaced.

ON THE FUTURE OF RINO
THE REAL CHALLENGE

Managing Growth

The challenge will not just be growth, but managing growth without erasing what made RiNo distinct in the first place.

01

Preserve Creative Spaces

Intentionally protect creative production spaces so artists can continue working in the district that they helped build.

02

Active Ground Floors

Require street-level activation that keeps the neighborhood vibrant, walkable, and full of character at all hours.

03

Affordable Housing

Secure affordable housing so artists and working residents are not pushed out as land values rise around them.
